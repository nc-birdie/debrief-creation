### Sales cycle is long, phased, and consultative

The company sells complex, highly engineered technology with a long, phased, deeply consultative sales cycle. Sales calls are essentially 1.5-hour education sessions because the technology is so fundamentally different that no customer knows how to evaluate it out of the box.

*Source: Transcription_summary_brief.docx*

### Feasibility study is first sales phase

The first phase is a Feasibility Study: a high-level initial assessment to see if a project makes sense. It provides 'key figures' (output, footprint, rough costs). Early proposals have a ±50% cost margin.

*Source: Transcription_summary_brief.docx*

### Deeper studies refine cost estimates to ±20%

After feasibility, deeper studies bring cost estimates closer to reality (±20% margin). These studies explore different scenarios to find the most profitable path for the customer.

*Source: Transcription_summary_brief.docx*

### Basic engineering follows successful studies

If the deeper studies look promising, the company performs basic engineering before the customer reaches a Final Investment Decision (FID).

*Source: Transcription_summary_brief.docx*

### FID is the ultimate customer decision hurdle

The Final Investment Decision (FID) is the ultimate customer hurdle. The project only materializes if the customer takes FID. The company currently has several clients stuck in the waiting phase just before FID.

*Source: Transcription_summary_brief.docx*

### EPC phase follows FID with modular delivery option

After FID, an EPC (Engineering, Procurement, Construction) contractor takes over to build the project. Sometimes the company works up to EPF (Engineering, Procurement, Fabrication) by delivering modular units or 'skids', but they traditionally avoid the final construction phase.

*Source: Transcription_summary_brief.docx*

### Four revenue pillars: studies, licensing, hardware, catalysts

The company has four revenue streams: (1) Paid Studies — charging engineering hours for early studies to generate short-term cash flow (done for free if the project is highly strategic); (2) Licensing — customers buy a license to use the company's IP; (3) Hardware — selling proprietary physical equipment (especially for e-React tech, which requires more proprietary hardware than older tech); (4) Catalysts — a recurring revenue stream.

*Source: Transcription_summary_brief.docx*

### Catalyst sales create recurring revenue lock-in

The first load of catalyst is usually locked to the company, but after that, customers can technically buy replacements from competitors. This creates a recurring revenue stream with initial lock-in.

### Cost competitiveness depends on power vs. gas price ratio

eREACT's levelized cost of product depends heavily on the ratio of electricity price to natural gas/biogas price. In markets with low electricity prices and high gas prices, eREACT is most cost-competitive. When costs are comparable, eREACT still offers benefits like lower carbon intensity, higher feedstock flexibility, and easier O&M. The business case improves further when carbon taxation is introduced.

### Consideration stage: Buyers use BETA framework and multi-criteria evaluation

In the consideration stage for chemical technology projects, buyers apply a structured BETA (Business, Engineering & Technology Assessment) evaluation framework. This includes: (1) Business Assessment—market conditions, financial forecasts, commercial potential, LCOP modeling; (2) Engineering Assessment—technical options, process configurations, integration with existing infrastructure; (3) Technology Assessment—technology readiness level (TRL), maturity, innovation potential, operational track record. Evaluation criteria weighting typically uses Multi-Criteria Decision Making (MCDM) approaches. Key criteria include: capital cost (CAPEX), operating cost (OPEX including energy/catalyst costs), technology readiness level, carbon intensity/emissions profile, scalability and modularity, vendor track record and references, catalyst lifetime and performance guarantees, and integration complexity with existing plant infrastructure. The 'good practice' principle is to not start engineering anything until the best process technology is decided.

### Decision stage: FID requires bankable FEED package with stress-tested economics

The Final Investment Decision (FID) is the formal approval from senior management to commit capital. FID assessment examines: (1) Project planning robustness—comprehensive scope, technical specifications, construction timelines, and cost estimates stress-tested by senior management; (2) Risk assessment—technical feasibility, market conditions, financing availability, regulatory compliance, legal challenges; (3) Financial viability—funding sources, capital structure (debt-equity ratio), insurance requirements, ROI potential. FID requires completed FEED deliverables including detailed scope of work, basic engineering documents, technical specifications, robust cost estimate (typically ±10%), and suggested procurement packages. For eReact, helping customers build FID-ready packages—including bankable cost estimates, risk mitigation plans, and offtake agreement support—accelerates the decision stage. The technology licensor's willingness to provide performance guarantees and reference data is critical at this gate.

### Buying journey is non-linear with six concurrent 'buying jobs'

Gartner research shows B2B buying doesn't follow a linear funnel. Instead, buyers engage in six concurrent 'buying jobs' that loop and revisit: (1) Problem identification—'We need to do something' (e.g., decarbonize methanol production); (2) Solution exploration—'What's out there?' (e.g., eReact vs. electrolysis vs. e-SMR); (3) Requirements building—'What exactly do we need?' (e.g., capacity, feedstock flexibility, integration needs); (4) Supplier selection—'Does this do what we want?'; (5) Validation—'We think we know the answer but need to be sure' (e.g., reference visits, pilot data); (6) Consensus creation—'We need everyone on board.' These jobs happen simultaneously, not sequentially—Gartner describes it as looking like 'a big bowl of spaghetti.' For eReact, this means content and sales enablement must support all six jobs at any time, not assume buyers move linearly. The consensus creation job is particularly critical given 6-10 decision makers in capital projects, each independently gathering 4-5 pieces of information that must be reconciled.

### FID financing dynamics create a 'first-mover licensor advantage' that reinforces lock-in

### Technology licensor selection criteria: proven scale, CAPEX, and catalyst lock-in

When selecting technology licensors for methanol and related chemical processes, project developers evaluate: (1) Proven technology at scale — Sypox claims 80,000+ hours of testing but their first 10 MW commercial unit is scheduled for 2026 delivery, creating a maturity gap Topsoe can exploit; (2) CAPEX and OPEX optimization — reactor technologies are compared on capital cost, operating cost, and carbon intensity trade-offs; (3) Catalyst performance and stability — catalyst selectivity, lifespan, and supply security are critical, with catalyst sales creating recurring revenue lock-in for the licensor; (4) Vendor track record — Topsoe is recognized as one of three dominant licensors in ammonia and is established in methanol, providing credibility advantage; (5) Integration flexibility — the ability to integrate into existing plants via revamp (a known eReact value proposition) reduces deployment risk; (6) Engineering support from feasibility through commissioning. The fierce competition between licensors means technology differentiation through reference projects and demonstrated performance is essential.

### Balance sheet vs. project finance distinction shapes customer qualification

A critical ICP dimension is how target customers finance capital-intensive projects like eREACT installations. 'Balance sheet customers' (corporate finance model) fund projects from their own equity and corporate debt — the project sits on the company's balance sheet, protecting simplicity but exposing the parent's credit profile. These are preferred by Topsoe because they can move faster through feasibility studies to FID, and their creditworthiness de-risks Topsoe's own project pipeline. Examples: Aramco, major refiners, large ammonia producers. 'Project finance customers' use Special Purpose Vehicles (SPVs) where debt is repaid solely from project cash flows, keeping liabilities off the parent's balance sheet. This is common among e-fuels startups and project developers (e.g., Arcadia eFuels). While project finance enables larger-scale investments and attracts infrastructure capital (KGAL, SWEN), it significantly lengthens the sales cycle due to lender due diligence, bankability requirements, and offtake agreement negotiations. Topsoe's preference for 'balance sheet customers' suggests targeting companies with investment-grade credit ratings and annual revenues sufficient to absorb 3-50 MW projects ($50M–$500M+ capex) without SPV structures.

### Key factors driving technology provider switching decisions in electrified methanol

### Market dynamics framework: regulatory push vs. economic pull creates an FID valley of death

The electrified reforming/green methanol market operates on a dual-driver model with significant tension: REGULATORY PUSH is strong (EU mandates, IMO framework, national incentives like Germany's €130M DLR funding, US 45V credits) but ECONOMIC PULL remains weak (green methanol 3x cost of conventional, grey hydrogen still cheaper without carbon pricing). This creates an 'FID valley of death' where projects stall between announcement and investment decision. Market dynamics are further shaped by: (1) Power price sensitivity—eREACT economics depend heavily on renewable electricity costs vs. natural gas prices; (2) Geographic arbitrage—China dominates construction (90%+ of capacity under construction) due to lower costs, while Europe leads in e-methanol project announcements; (3) Technology pathway competition—electrolysis dominates new project pipeline (51%) but existing reforming infrastructure (60% of operational capacity) creates a brownfield opportunity for eREACT; (4) Offtake uncertainty—shipping companies reluctant to sign long-term green methanol contracts at premium prices; (5) Long sales cycles—feasibility study → FEED → FID → EPC spans years, requiring sustained engagement.

### Multi-layered switching barriers in electrified reactor technology stack

Switching technology providers in the electrified methanol space involves at least five distinct barrier layers: (1) Catalyst lock-in—reforming catalysts are replaced every 6-10 years in fixed-bed systems, and switching catalyst suppliers mid-lifecycle requires requalification and voids performance guarantees; (2) Licensing IP—the reactor design, process parameters, and operating procedures are proprietary to the licensor, making mid-project provider changes effectively impossible; (3) Reactor hardware—Topsoe designs and manufactures the catalyst bed and reactor internals as proprietary equipment with 2,000+ internals installed across 700+ units globally; (4) Engineering integration—the licensed technology is deeply integrated with downstream process steps (methanol synthesis loop, purification), and changing one component cascades through the entire plant design; (5) Performance guarantees—in roughly 10-15% of projects, performance fails to meet guarantees, triggering renegotiations. Switching licensors would forfeit existing guarantee frameworks.

### Decarbonization timelines vs. executive tenure creates a personal urgency gap

A critical insight from McKinsey and ENGIE research: decarbonization investment timelines 'often stretch beyond the expected tenure of current company executives,' creating a fundamental misalignment between who makes the decision and who benefits from the outcome. This means individual decision-makers face a deeply personal calculus: committing capital today for benefits that may materialize after they've moved on. This creates two opposing emotional drivers—(1) fear of being held accountable for a costly project that doesn't pay off during their watch, and (2) desire to be the visionary leader who initiated a transformative project. For eReact campaigns, this means messaging must emphasize near-term, tangible milestones: feasibility study results within months, demonstration plant data available now (DLR Leuna plant), and a phased investment approach that delivers visible progress within a typical 3-5 year executive tenure.

### Self-serve vs. sales-assisted research patterns at each stage

Research method preferences shift across the journey: Awareness Stage (predominantly self-serve): Buyers conduct independent online research via search engines, LinkedIn, trade publications, and AI tools. 1-in-3 complete research before any vendor contact. Content must be publicly accessible and SEO-optimized. Conference presentations and peer discussions also drive awareness. Consideration Stage (hybrid self-serve + sales-assisted): Buyers request feasibility studies and technical consultations. The known 1.5-hour educational sales call format is typical here. Buyers also independently access white papers, webinars, and comparison tools. Pre-recorded technical deep-dives allow self-paced learning before sales engagement. Decision Stage (primarily sales-assisted): Deep technical collaboration during FEED phase. Reference site visits, detailed commercial negotiations, and performance guarantee discussions require direct sales involvement. However, 84% of buyers still expect self-service tools (LCOP calculators, configuration tools) even at this stage. Post-FID (fully sales-assisted): EPC execution, catalyst supply, and ongoing technical support are relationship-driven. The implication: Topsoe needs robust self-serve digital content for early stages while maintaining deep technical sales capability for later stages.
